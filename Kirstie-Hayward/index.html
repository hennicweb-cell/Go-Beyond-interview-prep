STAR Method — How to Structure Your Answers

The STAR framework — your guide for every competency question

SSituationSet the scene briefly. What was the context? What charity, what programme, what was the starting point?
TTaskWhat did you need to do? What was the challenge, the objective, the problem to solve?
AActionWhat did YOU specifically do? Not "we" — "I". Be concrete and specific about your personal contribution.
RResultWhat happened? What was the outcome? Give a number wherever you can. What did you learn?

Aim for 3–4 minutes per STAR answer. Practice out loud before the day — the difference between a prepared and an unprepared STAR answer is enormous. Don't read from notes; tell the story.

📢 "Tell me about a time you owned a fundraising campaign end-to-end where something went wrong" Suggested: Herts & Middlesex direct mail + fulfilment error
S
At Herts & Middlesex Wildlife Trust, we ran regular direct mail appeals — typically two to three per year. I managed the whole process: briefing the designer, coordinating with the mailing house, writing copy, segmenting the database, and tracking results as donations came in.
T
One appeal launched and after two weeks I was seeing almost no donations and far fewer return-to-senders than usual. Something wasn't right, but I couldn't immediately identify what.
A
I looked at the data carefully — the donations and returns were all coming from one segment only, which told me immediately that the other segments hadn't received their packs. I contacted the fulfilment company, who investigated and confirmed they had only mailed one segment due to a processing error. They apologised and funded a full reprint and resend at their own cost. I also put a donor notification in place to manage anyone who had been expecting to receive the pack.
R
The appeal was rescued and went out to all intended segments. The data pattern is what saved us — if I hadn't been monitoring closely and spotted that anomaly, we might have written the whole appeal off as a failure. From then on, I built a post-dispatch check into every direct mail process to verify all segments had been sent within 48 hours of the mailing date.

📢 "How would you build a regular giving acquisition programme from nothing with no ring-fenced budget?" Structured approach — not STAR, but needs to be concrete
First
Understand what you already have. Pull the data on the 200–300 existing regular givers: where did they come from? Volunteers, ICM donors, challenge event participants, alumni? What's the current retention rate? Attrition? That tells you both what to protect and what acquisition channel is most likely to work.
Then
Use Ice Cream Moments as the entry point. One-off ICM donors at £3.50 are the warmest possible acquisition prospect. They've already given, they understand the mission, and they've opted in to communications. A conversion journey from ICM one-off to monthly regular giver is the cheapest, most persuasive acquisition route available — and the ask is small (just keep giving what you gave once, every month). I'd build the case for this as a pilot programme with a specific conversion target and a clear ROI calculation based on lifetime value.
Then
Test a telephone welcome call to converted regular givers from the first campaign — using staff and volunteers during a quiet operations period. This boosts retention in the critical first three months (where attrition is highest) at almost no cost.
Measure
Track conversion rate from ICM donors, first-month retention, and three-month retention. Report to Sara monthly. Build the business case for a larger acquisition campaign once you've proven the model works at small scale. Year-one ROI from regular giving acquisition is typically break-even or slightly negative — what you're investing in is lifetime value. Sara needs to understand that framing before approving spend.
📢 "How would you launch Go Beyond's legacy programme? What's the most common mistake charities make?" Draw on KEEN's six-year legacy strategy — your most distinctive credential
First steps
Join Remember a Charity — it provides a focal point (Legacy Week in September, Wills Week in March) and lends credibility to a charity that is new to legacy conversations. Then audit what Go Beyond already has: any legacy enquirers recorded in the database? Existing donors who might be ready for a legacy conversation? What does the website legacy page look like?
Messaging
Keep it small and tangible. Don't ask supporters to leave their estate to Go Beyond — explain that even a small residual gift (after family and loved ones) could fund a child's break. Use the existing alumni story — 21,000 children have benefited since Go Beyond began. That history is the legacy argument: what you've built over 25+ years is worth protecting and continuing.
Trustees
Get the trustees behind the message. At KEEN this took time — some were uncomfortable with talking about death in a charity context. The answer is to reframe it: this is not about asking people to die for you, it's about giving supporters the opportunity to extend their lifetime commitment into their legacy. Once trustees understand this, they become ambassadors.
Most common mistake
Not recognising legacy pledgers differently in supporter journeys. Once someone tells you they've included you in their will, their entire supporter journey should change — they are your most committed supporter. They should receive a separate, more personal communications track. Treating them the same as any other donor is both a missed opportunity and a failure to honour the relationship.
