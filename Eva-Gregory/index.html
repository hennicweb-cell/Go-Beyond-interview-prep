STAR Method — How to Structure Your Answers

The STAR framework — your guide for every competency question

SSituationSet the scene briefly. What was the context? What charity, what programme, what was the starting point?
TTaskWhat did you need to do? What was the challenge, the objective, the problem to solve?
AActionWhat did YOU specifically do? Not "we" — "I". Be concrete and specific about your personal contribution.
RResultWhat happened? What was the outcome? Give a number wherever you can. What did you learn?

Aim for 3–4 minutes per STAR answer. Practice out loud before the day — the difference between a prepared and an unprepared STAR answer is enormous. Don't read from notes; tell the story.

📢 "Tell me about a campaign you owned end-to-end where something went wrong" Suggested: The charity raffle
S
At SarcoidosisUK, shop income was limited and I could see that most of what we raised came from event tickets. I wanted to find a way to boost income, re-engage existing supporters, and bring people to the shop to see what else we offered — in a way that felt fun rather than just another ask.
T
I decided to run a raffle — which turned out to involve considerably more planning than I'd initially anticipated, including applying for a local authority licence, sourcing prizes entirely without a budget, and building the whole thing myself.
A
I applied for and obtained the council licence (£40, and they took their time). I approached companies and donors I already had relationships with to secure 14 prizes — including a signed Arsenal ball, a Fortnum & Mason hamper, and football match tickets. I built the shop product, wrote all the marketing copy, launched across email and social media, and then managed the crisis when raffle confirmation emails started going to spam folders. I tested from multiple email addresses including personal ones to diagnose the problem, added a prominent note on the shop page telling supporters to check their spam, kept calm while fielding emails asking if it was a scam, and kept the campaign moving.
R
We sold over 1,000 tickets and raised a meaningful amount from a £40 investment. The spam issue affected fewer people than I'd initially feared — but the learning was clear: just because an email comes through for you doesn't mean it's coming through for everyone. I now build a spam-check step into every email campaign before launch.
📢 "Tell me about a time you built income from very little" Suggested: Support services to donations pipeline
S
At SarcoidosisUK, we had a large group of people who had used our support services — the nurse helpline, support group meetings — who had never been asked to donate. They were on our database, they had consented to contact, and they had a direct relationship with the charity. But there was no pathway from support user to donor.
T
I wanted to create a gentle, appropriate income pipeline from this group without compromising the integrity of the support services or making people feel that help came with a price attached.
A
I worked with our Support Services Officer to rework the feedback emails she was already sending after helpline calls and support group attendance. I wrote three different message variants — different tones, different levels of directness — and she sent them to different segments. I tracked which variant generated the most donations by comparing the data from the feedback forms with the donation records. I then combined the most effective elements of all three into a single optimised approach.
R
Conversion from support service contact to donation moved from under 5% to approaching 50% of feedback forms generating a donation. That is income from a source that had never been activated before, at zero cost, using communication infrastructure that already existed. The result came from testing, data comparison, and iteration — not from spending anything.

📢 "How would you build a regular giver acquisition programme with no ring-fenced budget?" Structured approach — not STAR, but needs to be specific and income-led
First
Start with the data. Pull a segment of one-off donors who have given more than once in the last two years — people who have already decided Go Beyond is worth supporting. That is the warm conversion pool. The ask is not "become a regular giver" cold — it's "you've already given us twice, could you make that monthly?" The cost is an email. The conversion rate should be significantly higher than cold acquisition.
Segment
Within that warm pool, segment by giving amount. Donors who have given £10-50 get a story and a £5/month ask. Donors who have given £100-200 get a more personal email with a stronger impact case and a £15-20/month ask. Use the ICM campaign as the entry point — someone who donated £3.50 for an ice cream could be asked to give a break every year for just £3.50 a month. Small, tangible, campaign-linked.
Business case
If Go Beyond's 200-300 current regular givers generate £90,000 — that's an average of £300-450 per giver per year, or around £25-37/month. The lifetime value of a retained regular giver over three years is £900-1,350. I would use those numbers to justify even a small paid spend (targeted Meta ads to existing email subscribers or social followers) once I've proven warm conversion works first.
Measure
Track warm conversion rate, first-month retention, and three-month retention. Report to Sara monthly against those KPIs. Retention improvement is cheaper than acquisition — even a 10% improvement in retention across the existing 200-300 regular givers adds meaningful income without any acquisition spend at all.
